Online Safety

Introduction to child safety online

Technology is continually changing and will be there for a while, yet many do not consider the security risk. A disturbing statistic from an online research is that 74% of parents are unaware of their children's internet habits. Additionally, internet activity grows yearly. 85% of moms reported using technology to occupy their kids.

Many young people consider the virtual world to be more real than the physical one. Understanding what our children view online, what is out there, both good and bad, and how it affects their physical and mental well-being is vital for their welfare.

The issue is that, as many of us would gladly acknowledge, we feel as though we don't really comprehend the internet world. Even without discussing 4chan and the dark web, Twitter, Instagram, and Snapchat are confusing enough. In addition, we don't think we have the technological know-how to negotiate this challenging environment. You are the greatest person to protect your child's internet safety. Building a healthy and trusting connection with your child via discussions about internet safety is a great way to gain their trust. Establish clear guidelines for what and when they may access the internet, but also be there for your kids when they mess up or go too far. Isn't that what parenthood is really all about?

Cyber safety guidelines for parents

Never give out identifying information

Consider using a pseudonym or unlisting your child's name if your service allows it.

Never let a youngster to meet up in person with another internet user without parental consent. If a meeting is scheduled, have the first one in a public area, and make sure your youngster is there.

Learn about the services that your kid utilizes. Ask your youngster to show you how to log in if you're not sure how. Find out what kinds of information are available and whether there are any controls that allow parents to ban inappropriate content.

Never reply to comments or posts on the bulletin board that are provocative, vulgar, hostile, threatening, or otherwise uncomfortable for you. Encourage your kids to let you know if they come across such communications

Establish clear limitations and limits for your kids' computer use. As a reminder, talk about these guidelines and place them close to the computer.

Instead of the child's bedroom, think about keeping the computer there. Learn as much about their "online friends" as you do about all of their other friends.
